# The Bookkeeper Role — Complete Reference

> **Scope:** A bookkeeper's job is to *record, organise, and reconcile* financial transactions so the business always knows exactly what happened with its money. They do **not** file taxes, run payroll compliance, give legal advice, or do financial planning — those belong to the accountant, payroll service, or CFO.

---

## Boundary: What a Bookkeeper Does vs Does Not Do

### Bookkeeper owns
- Recording every transaction
- Categorising expenses correctly
- Reconciling bank accounts
- Tracking who owes you money (accounts receivable)
- Tracking what you owe others (accounts payable)
- Producing P&L and basic reports
- Keeping records organised

### Out of scope — not bookkeeping
- Filing tax returns
- Calculating estimated taxes
- Running payroll and tax withholding
- Financial forecasting and planning
- Business strategy advice
- Audits and IRS representation
- Legal or compliance advice

## Daily Tasks

### 1. Collect and receive source documents
**Frequency:** Continuously throughout the day

Before anything can be recorded, evidence must exist. A bookkeeper cannot enter a transaction without a source document — a receipt, invoice, bank record, or note — that proves the transaction happened.

**Every source document type they handle:**
- **Sales receipts** — proof that a customer paid. POS receipt, PayPal confirmation, Venmo screenshot, Square receipt.
- **Purchase receipts** — proof of what the business spent. Gas station, hardware store, Amazon order confirmation, restaurant receipt.
- **Invoices sent** — the bill sent to a customer for work done or goods sold. Before the customer pays, this is the only record that money is expected.
- **Bills received** — invoices from suppliers, vendors, landlord, utilities. Proof of what the business owes before it pays.
- **Bank statements** — the authoritative record of what actually moved through the business bank account. Downloaded monthly.
- **Credit card statements** — same as bank statements but for business credit cards. Every card gets reconciled separately.
- **Contracts / agreements** — for large or recurring transactions, the signed contract is the source document proving the financial obligation.
- **Mileage log** — the IRS requires a written log (date, destination, purpose, miles) — a verbal estimate is not acceptable.
- **Cash payment records** — the hardest to track. Cash paid or received with no automatic trail requires the owner to manually record every cash transaction.

**How documents actually arrive (the messy reality):**
Paper receipts crumpled in a pocket. Photos texted to the bookkeeper. PDFs emailed. Forwarded bank notifications. Handwritten notes. Sometimes just a verbal "I spent about $200 at the supply store." The bookkeeper's first job is creating order from this chaos — and frequently asking the owner to find documents they've already lost.

---

### 2. Record (enter) transactions into the books
**Frequency:** Daily or every few days — never let it pile up

Every financial event gets a permanent entry in the books. The goal: every dollar that moved through the business can be traced back to a source document and a recorded entry.

**What every transaction entry must capture:**
- **Date** — the date the transaction actually happened, not when it was recorded. Critical for period accuracy.
- **Amount** — exact dollar amount. For split transactions (part business, part personal), only the business portion.
- **Payee or payer** — who the money went to (expense) or came from (income). "Home Depot" not just "hardware store."
- **Category** — the type of expense or income. This is the judgment call. Must align with IRS categories for deductibility.
- **Payment method** — cash, check, business card, personal card used for business, bank transfer, Venmo, Zelle.
- **Description / memo** — brief note on what it was for. "Lumber for Johnson job" not just "Home Depot purchase."
- **Receipt attached** — link or scan of the source document. IRS requires records be kept 3–7 years.

**The categorisation judgment — where skill matters:**

Categorisation is not mechanical. The same purchase can belong to different categories depending on context, and the wrong category affects tax deductions:

- Lunch at a restaurant: "Meals & Entertainment" (50% deductible) vs "Owner Draw" (not deductible) depending on whether it was a client meeting or personal
- New laptop: "Equipment" (depreciates over time) vs "Office Supplies" (expensed immediately) — different tax treatment
- Payment to a contractor: "Contract Labor" (requires 1099 if over $600) vs "Professional Fees" — different IRS reporting
- Home office portion of rent: "Rent" or "Home Office" — only valid if owner qualifies for the deduction
- Phone bill: "Utilities" or "Communication" — and only the business-use percentage

> **The mixed personal/business problem:** The #1 headache for bookkeepers working with SMBs — owners use personal cards for business and business cards for personal. Every statement has to be reviewed line by line, and the owner has to confirm which charges were business. This happens every single month and never gets better without a system.

**Tools used today:** QuickBooks Online, Xero, Wave (free), Excel / Google Sheets

### 3. Manage accounts payable — bills owed by the business
**Frequency:** As bills arrive, checked daily

Accounts payable (AP) is the list of bills the business has received but not yet paid. The bookkeeper logs each bill when it arrives, tracks the due date, and records the payment when it goes out. Goal: nothing gets paid late, nothing gets paid twice, nothing gets missed.

**Step by step:**
- **Bill arrives** — enter immediately: vendor name, amount, due date, what it's for. Do not wait until it needs to be paid.
- **Verify the bill** — does the amount match what was agreed? Is this a duplicate? Is this a vendor the business actually uses?
- **Schedule payment** — note the due date. Pay early enough to avoid late fees but not so early it creates a cash shortage.
- **Record the payment** — when paid, mark the bill closed. Record payment method and date. File the receipt.
- **Recurring bills** — rent, insurance, subscriptions, utilities. These repeat monthly. The bookkeeper often sets up auto-pay and confirms it records correctly each month.

**Typical bills a small business has monthly:** Rent or mortgage, utilities (electric, gas, water, internet, phone), insurance (liability, property, vehicle), software subscriptions, supplier invoices, loan payments, equipment leases. A busy SMB can have 15–30 recurring bills to track.

---

### 4. Manage accounts receivable — money owed to the business
**Frequency:** Daily tracking, weekly follow-up

Accounts receivable (AR) is the list of invoices the business has sent but not yet been paid for. This is money earned but not yet in the bank. The bookkeeper tracks every outstanding invoice and follows up with late customers — because late payments are a leading cause of SMB cash flow crises.

**Step by step:**
- **Invoice created and sent** — logged immediately: customer name, amount, due date (Net 15, Net 30, or Net 60), what work it's for.
- **Payment received** — record the payment, match it to the correct invoice, mark the invoice paid. Note if partial.
- **Invoice goes past due** — at 1 day past due, noted. At 7–14 days, polite reminder sent. At 30 days, escalated to owner.
- **Aging report** — weekly snapshot showing all outstanding invoices sorted by age: current, 1–30 days, 31–60 days, 61–90 days, 90+ days.

**The human complexity layer:**
- Customer says they paid but bookkeeper can't find it — have to reconcile and trace
- Customer disputes the invoice amount — flag to owner, bookkeeper can't resolve alone
- Customer pays wrong amount — apply partial payment or wait for full?
- Customer has gone silent — when does this become a bad debt write-off?

> **Why this matters more than most owners realise:** A contractor who billed $50,000 in a month but has $35,000 unpaid can still run out of cash. Profitable on paper, broke in the bank. Tracking receivables isn't just bookkeeping — it's survival for cash-flow-sensitive businesses like contractors, consultants, and service businesses.

## Weekly Tasks

### 1. Review and categorise bank feed transactions
**Frequency:** 2–3 times per week

Most bookkeeping software connects directly to the business bank account and pulls transactions automatically. The bookkeeper's job: review each imported transaction, confirm or correct the category, attach the receipt, and approve it.

**What the bookkeeper does for each imported transaction:**
- Review the merchant name and amount — does this look right? Recognised?
- Confirm or change the auto-suggested category (software guesses, bookkeeper verifies)
- Add a description / memo if the merchant name isn't self-explanatory
- Attach the matching receipt or invoice
- Flag any unrecognised transaction — ask the owner
- Split transactions that are part business, part personal
- Mark transfers between accounts (moving money from savings to checking is not income or expense)

> **The "I don't know what this is" pile:** Every week there are transactions the bookkeeper can't categorise without asking the owner. These pile up if the owner doesn't respond quickly. Unanswered questions = uncategorised transactions = books that can't be closed.

---

### 2. Chase the owner for missing information
**Frequency:** Every week — the most time-consuming hidden task

A bookkeeper working with an SMB owner spends 30–40% of their time not doing bookkeeping but waiting for the owner to provide missing pieces. This is the single biggest operational friction in the bookkeeper-owner relationship.

**What they're chasing, specifically:**
- **Missing receipts** — "I see a $240 charge at Sherwin-Williams but I don't have a receipt. Can you find it?" The owner often can't.
- **Business vs personal clarification** — "Was this $180 dinner business or personal?" Owner has to remember a meal from 3 weeks ago.
- **Context on unusual purchases** — "What was the $650 at Northern Tool for?" (Equipment? Supplies? A gift?)
- **Cash transactions** — "You mentioned paying $300 cash for labor — do you remember the date and who it was?"
- **Contractor information** — "You paid Miguel $800 this month — is he a contractor? I'll need his address and SSN if we pay him more than $600 this year."
- **Customer payment details** — "I see a $1,500 Zelle from 'Dave' — which invoice does this apply to?"

> **Why this is such a painful cycle:** Owners are busy running their business. Bookkeeping questions feel low priority. They respond days later, often with incomplete answers, then the bookkeeper has to follow up again. Meanwhile the books can't be closed. This friction is why many SMBs end up with books that are months behind.

---

### 3. Monitor cash position
**Frequency:** Weekly — often the owner's #1 question

The bookkeeper keeps a running picture of the business's cash health — not just what's in the bank right now, but what's coming in (receivables due) and what's going out (bills due) over the next 2–4 weeks.

- Current bank balance (from the bank feed, real-time)
- Outstanding receivables — money expected in and when
- Bills due in the next 2 weeks — money going out and when
- Projected cash position in 2 weeks: bank + receivables − bills due
- Flag if the projected position goes below a safe threshold — alert the owner early

> **The question behind the question:** When an owner asks "how are we doing?" they almost always mean "do we have enough cash to cover what's coming?" A bookkeeper who can answer this clearly — and proactively, before being asked — is invaluable.

## Monthly Tasks

### 1. Bank reconciliation
**Frequency:** Once a month — the most critical bookkeeping task

Reconciliation is the process of proving that the books match the bank statement, dollar for dollar, for every single transaction in the month. It's the bookkeeper's quality control check.

**Step by step:**
1. **Download the bank statement** — the official record from the bank for the full calendar month. This is the ground truth.
2. **Match every line** — go through every transaction on the bank statement and find its matching entry in the books.
3. **Find unmatched items** — anything on the bank statement not in the books (missed entry) or in the books not on the statement (timing difference or error).
4. **Explain every difference** — each unmatched item must be resolved: add the missing entry, delete the duplicate, or note as a legitimate timing difference.
5. **Confirm ending balance** — when all differences are resolved, the book balance must equal the bank statement balance exactly.
6. **Repeat for every account** — every business bank account AND every business credit card gets reconciled separately. A business with 2 bank accounts and 3 credit cards has 5 reconciliations each month.

> **Why this is harder than it sounds:** Timing differences make reconciliation tricky. A check written on March 29 might not clear until April 3. One penny off means you haven't reconciled. It matters that much.

> **What happens when SMBs skip reconciliation:** Errors compound month over month. A missed $50 entry in January becomes a mysterious discrepancy haunting every reconciliation for the rest of the year. By December, the books can be hundreds or thousands of dollars off with no way to know where it went wrong.

---

### 2. Produce the monthly Profit & Loss report
**Frequency:** After reconciliation is complete

The Profit & Loss (P&L) — also called Income Statement — shows how the business performed financially in a given period. The bookkeeper produces this after every monthly close.

**What the P&L contains:**
- **Revenue / Income** — total money earned from the business's primary activity
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** — direct costs of delivering the product or service: materials, direct labor, subcontractors
- **Gross Profit** — Revenue minus COGS
- **Operating Expenses** — all overhead: rent, utilities, insurance, office supplies, marketing, software, vehicle expenses, meals, professional fees
- **Net Profit (or Loss)** — Gross Profit minus Operating Expenses. The bottom line.

**How the bookkeeper delivers this:**
- Export from accounting software as PDF or share a live link
- Include a brief written note explaining anything notable — big changes vs last month, unusual items
- Compare to last month and same month last year

> **The translation gap — the biggest opportunity:** Most owners receive a P&L and don't know what to do with it. They look at the bottom line and ignore everything else. A bookkeeper who can translate the P&L into plain language — "your materials costs went up 22% this month, which is why your profit is down even though your revenue is up" — is worth far more than one who just sends a PDF.

---

### 3. Review and clean up uncategorised / flagged items
**Frequency:** Before books can be closed each month

Before the month can be officially "closed," every transaction must be categorised and every flagged item resolved.

- Resolve all items flagged as "ask owner" during the month
- Categorise any remaining transactions in the "Uncategorised" bucket
- Split any transactions that need to be divided across categories
- Confirm no transactions are missing (compare to bank statement count)
- Make sure every transaction has a description — not just a merchant name

> **Books are "closed" only when this is done.** A month is only finished when every transaction is categorised, every question is answered, and reconciliation is complete. Until then, the P&L is inaccurate.

## Year-End Tasks

### 1. Full year review and cleanup
**Frequency:** December–January every year

Before handing off to the accountant for tax filing, the bookkeeper does a full review of the entire year's records — finding errors, fixing miscategorisations, and ensuring every month's reconciliation is complete.

- Review all 12 months for uncategorised or miscategorised transactions
- Confirm all 12 bank and credit card reconciliations are complete
- Check for any duplicate entries across the year
- Verify all receivables are marked paid or written off as bad debt
- Verify all payables are marked paid or still outstanding
- Make sure owner draws / distributions are correctly recorded (not as expenses)
- Separate any personal expenses that accidentally went through business accounts

> **The shoebox scenario — what your product must prevent:** When a business hasn't kept up with bookkeeping, everything lands on the bookkeeper in January as a box of receipts and unreconciled statements. Reconstructing 12 months of records from scratch can take 20–40 hours. Accountants charge $150–400/hr for this cleanup. Completely avoidable with consistent monthly habits.

---

### 2. Prepare books for the accountant handoff
**Frequency:** January–March every year

The bookkeeper's year-end output is what the accountant uses to file the tax return. Clean, complete books = fast, cheap tax filing. Messy books = the accountant spends expensive hours cleaning up what the bookkeeper should have done.

- Produce final annual P&L and Balance Sheet
- Provide list of all fixed assets purchased in the year (for depreciation)
- Provide list of all contractors paid $600+ (for 1099 reference — bookkeeper identifies, accountant or owner files)
- Note any unusual or one-time items the accountant should know about
- Grant accountant access to the accounting software
- Respond to accountant questions about specific transactions

> **The ROI of clean books:** A business with clean books handed to an accountant in January typically pays $500–1,500 for tax preparation. The same business handing over a mess in March pays $2,000–5,000 for cleanup + filing — and often files late. Clean books is a direct, measurable cost saving.

## Always-On Responsibilities

### 1. Be the owner's financial memory
**Frequency:** Whenever the owner asks — multiple times a week

The most important always-on role is being the person who can answer any financial question about the business instantly, accurately, and in plain language.

**Questions they field constantly:**
- "How much money do we have right now?"
- "Did Johnson pay us for the March job?"
- "How much have we spent on materials this year?"
- "Are we doing better or worse than last month?"
- "Can I afford to buy a new truck?"
- "What's my biggest expense I could cut?"
- "How much profit did I actually make last quarter?"

> **This is the role worth reimagining:** A human bookkeeper can only answer these questions during business hours, with a delay, and at $30–80/hr. The data to answer all of these questions is already in the books. The question is whether anyone can surface it instantly, at any time, in plain English.

---

### 2. Maintain the paper trail
**Frequency:** Ongoing — every transaction, every time

Every transaction the business ever makes should be traceable back to a source document. The IRS can request records going back 3–6 years.

- Attach a receipt or document to every transaction in the books
- Store documents in a logical, consistent filing system (physical or digital)
- Keep records for at least 3 years (7 years for assets)
- Be able to pull any document on request — from the owner, accountant, or lender

> **Why this is harder than it sounds:** Paper receipts fade, get lost, or are never kept. Email receipts get deleted. Building a habit of document capture at the moment of transaction is the only long-term solution — and it requires changing owner behaviour, not just bookkeeper process.

---

### 3. Flag anomalies and protect against errors
**Frequency:** Every time records are reviewed

A good bookkeeper doesn't just record what they're told — they notice when something doesn't look right and flag it before it becomes a bigger problem.

- Duplicate charges — same vendor, same amount, same week
- Unusual amounts — a $4,000 charge from a vendor who usually bills $400
- Unexpected cash withdrawals that don't match any known business expense
- Missing income — a regular customer who hasn't paid when they usually do
- Expense categories trending up fast — materials costs 40% higher than same period last year
- Subscriptions for services the business no longer uses — waste hiding in plain sight

> **The trust this builds:** When a bookkeeper catches a duplicate charge or notices a vendor overcharged, the owner's trust grows dramatically. It signals "someone is watching out for me." This protective quality is what makes owners loyal to a good bookkeeper for years — and what makes them tell others about them.
