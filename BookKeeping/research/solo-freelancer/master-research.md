## The Narrative in One Page

**The market is real and underserved.**
72.7M Americans work independently. Of those, ~16.6M are materially self-employed by BLS standards. Penny's addressable slice — US sole proprietors, $40K–$150K revenue, doing their own books — is conservatively 10–15M people. The median full-time freelancer earns ~$85K. The market is not declining; it is growing toward a projected 90M by 2028.

**They are flying blind financially.**
90% didn't know quarterly taxes were required. 74% aren't getting paid on time. 59% are owed $50,000+ in outstanding invoices. 56% don't know what deductions they qualify for. Over 70% spend 20 hours filing taxes — scrambling at year-end instead of tracking year-round. 60% of freelancers started without a budget or financial plan.

**The tools available do not serve them.**
Only 40% use dedicated accounting software. 60% are on Word docs, templates, or notes apps. The tools that exist (QuickBooks, FreshBooks) were built for small businesses with employees — freelancers are an afterthought. Accountants and bookkeepers themselves say the software is too complicated for small business owners.

**The pain is the same but the flavour differs by segment.**
Every segment wants to know: *"Am I okay financially?"* But what's making them not okay differs. Creatives drown in small invoices and miss April. Developers hit the S-Corp threshold with no signal. Consultants travel everywhere and can't prove it to a CPA. Coaches collect payments from five apps and can't reconcile any of them.

**A CPA can fix most of this — if the client arrives with the right records.**
They almost never do. The quality of records a freelancer brings directly determines both the quality of tax advice they receive and the cost of CPA time. Clean, complete, correctly categorised books mean the CPA spends time on strategy — S-Corp elections, depreciation choices, retirement accounts — not archaeology.

## PART 4: THE CPA LENS

*A CPA working with each segment needs specific, complete, correctly formatted information to deliver maximum value.*
*The quality of what a freelancer brings to a CPA determines both the quality of advice and the size of the CPA's bill.*

### The CPA's Core Problem With Freelancer Clients

A CPA charges for time. When a freelancer arrives with a shoebox of receipts, five PayPal statements, and a vague recollection of what they spent on Adobe CC, the CPA spends 60–70% of their time on *data archaeology* — reconciling, categorising, and chasing records — and 30% on actual tax strategy. The freelancer pays a high bill for low-value work.

When a freelancer arrives with clean, categorised, reconciled records — every income source confirmed, every expense categorised with business purpose, all 1099s received and issued — the CPA spends 80% of their time on strategy: S-Corp election timing, depreciation choices, retirement account options, entity structure. The freelancer pays a similar or lower bill for high-value work.

**The stat:** Basic freelancer Schedule C tax prep costs $400–$1,200/year (CPA time). S-Corp returns cost $1,500–$4,000+. The difference in quality of advice is not about the CPA — it's about the quality of records the client brings.
Source: via [selfemployed.com](https://www.selfemployed.com/bookkeeping-self-employed-complete-guide/) 🔵 SECONDARY

### 4.1 What a CPA Needs From Creatives (S1)

Creatives bring the messiest records of any segment — high transaction volume, many clients, receipts in inboxes and photo rolls.

**Income records — what a CPA needs:**
- All client invoices issued in the tax year (by client, amount, date sent, date paid)
- All 1099-NEC forms received from clients who paid $600+ (clients are required to send these)
- All platform income summaries (Stripe, PayPal, Upwork — annual statements)
- Any income not captured by 1099 (cash, bank transfer — needs bank statement reconciliation)

**Expense records — what a CPA needs:**
- Software subscriptions: **monthly amounts + business purpose** (Adobe CC, Figma, Webflow, Canva, stock subscriptions — all deductible 100%)
- Equipment purchases: **receipt + date + price** for any item over $2,500 (Section 179 election decision — full deduction vs. depreciation)
- Home office: **square footage of workspace** + **total home square footage** (to calculate deductible percentage)
- Internet + phone: **monthly bill** + **estimated business-use percentage**
- Professional development: receipts for courses, books, conferences
- Subcontractor payments: any individual paid $600+, their legal name, address, and EIN/SSN (for 1099-NEC that the creative must file)

*CPA friction point:* Without organised records, a creative's CPA spends the majority of billable time on data archaeology rather than tax strategy.

---

### 4.2 What a CPA Needs From Tech Freelancers (S2)

Tech freelancers have fewer transactions but more structural complexity — W2+1099 hybrid income, SaaS subscriptions that look like personal charges, and an S-Corp decision that has real tax year-end deadlines.

**Income records — what a CPA needs:**
- All W2 forms (if also employed)
- All 1099-NEC forms received from clients
- All invoices issued (even if no 1099 was sent — clients paying under $600 still need to be reported)
- Bank statements showing all business income deposits (to catch anything without a 1099)

**Expense records — what a CPA needs:**
- Full SaaS/software inventory: **tool name + monthly cost + business purpose** (IDEs, cloud hosting, GitHub, AI tools, project management — all deductible)
- Equipment: **MacBook, monitors, peripherals — purchase date + price + business-use percentage** (Section 179 if >50% business use)
- Home office: **sq footage of workspace** + **total home sq footage** + **whether space is used exclusively for work**
- Professional development: online courses, conference fees, technical books
- Any subcontractor payments made ($600+ → must file 1099-NEC)

**The S-Corp question — what a CPA specifically needs to advise:**
- **Net profit for the year** (total revenue minus all deductible expenses)
- **Prior year net profit** (to identify trend)
- **Whether a reasonable salary has been determined** (IRS requires this for S-Corp election)
- **State of incorporation** (S-Corp rules vary by state)
- **Deadline awareness:** S-Corp election (Form 2553) must be filed by **March 15** of the tax year it takes effect — or within 75 days of incorporation. Missing this costs a full year's savings.

> If net income is tracking toward $80K+ → CPA should be flagged before Q3, not at tax time.
> At $100K net: S-Corp saves $5K–$8K/year — [Lettuce.co](https://lettuce.co/resources/tax-savings-deductions-scorps-freelancers) ✅

*CPA friction point:* S-Corp decisions are most valuable when flagged before Q3, not at tax time. CPAs who serve tech freelancers consistently report that the S-Corp conversation happens too late — after the savings window has closed for the year.

### 4.3 What a CPA Needs From Independent Consultants (S3)

Consultants have the most financially complex records — travel, meals, subcontractors, and S-Corp decisions at higher income levels where the savings are transformative.

**Income records — what a CPA needs:**
- All client invoices and engagement letters (to establish business purpose of related expenses)
- All 1099-NEC forms received
- Any retainer agreements (to confirm revenue recognition treatment)
- Outstanding invoices (for accounts receivable disclosure)

**Expense records — what a CPA needs:**
- **Travel:** Every trip with: destination, business purpose, dates, flight/hotel/rental car receipts. IRS requires documentation of business purpose — "visited client" is insufficient; "strategy session with Acme Corp re: Q3 ops review" is sufficient.
- **Business meals:** Receipt + who attended + what was discussed. 50% deductible. Zero tolerance for "team dinner" without business purpose documented.
- **Client entertainment:** Do NOT include. Post-TCJA (2018), entertainment (tickets, golf, events) is **0% deductible**. Consultants who've been freelancing since before 2018 often still try to claim these — CPA will reject them. Any entertainment expenses must be excluded.
- **Subcontractor payments:** Full name, address, EIN/SSN, total paid. Any individual paid $600+ needs a 1099-NEC filed by Jan 31.
- **Home office:** sq footage + total home sq footage + lease/mortgage documentation (for actual method vs. simplified)
- **Professional memberships, publications, industry subscriptions:** All deductible

**The S-Corp conversation — what a CPA needs:**
- Net profit (at this income level, S-Corp savings are $10K–$20K+/year)
- "Reasonable salary" benchmarking (what does a strategy consultant with equivalent experience earn as an employee?)
- State-specific S-Corp requirements (some states have franchise taxes that reduce the benefit)
- Timeline: Form 2553 by March 15 of the election year

*CPA friction point:* Travel and meal documentation is the most time-intensive segment of consultant returns. Without it, the CPA either guesses or loses the deduction. A consultant with active travel and subcontractor payments creates the most paperwork-intensive case of any segment.

---

### 4.4 What a CPA Needs From Service Solopreneurs (S4)

The defining challenge: income arrives from 4–6 platforms simultaneously. A CPA cannot begin to prepare a return without knowing the total from each platform — and the platforms don't always agree with the bank.

**Income records — what a CPA needs:**
- **Stripe annual summary** (total charges + refunds + fees)
- **PayPal annual summary** (same format — gross income, not net-of-fees)
- **Venmo Business annual statement** (if applicable)
- **Square annual sales report**
- **Bank statements** — to reconcile against all platform summaries (platforms report to IRS; bank confirms what actually landed)
- **1099-K forms** from any platform that processed $5,000+ in 2024 (Stripe, PayPal, Square will send these)
- **Manual income log** for any cash, Zelle, or check payments not captured by platform 1099-Ks

> Critical IRS rule: A 1099-K shows *gross* platform income — not net of fees. CPA must reconcile gross reported to IRS with net received in bank, then deduct platform fees as a business expense. Failure to do this leads to overstating taxable income.

**Expense records — what a CPA needs:**
- **Platform fees** (Stripe ~2.9% + $0.30 per transaction, PayPal ~2.9%, Square ~2.6%) — all deductible as "payment processing fees"
- **Home office / client room:** sq footage of space used exclusively for client sessions + total home sq footage. If the space is also a guest room, the exclusive-use test fails — CPA cannot claim it.
- **Professional liability insurance** premiums — fully deductible
- **Continuing education and certification** costs (ICF renewal, CPD, licensing fees)
- **Scheduling / practice management software** (e.g. Calendly, Simple Practice, Jane App) — fully deductible
- **Health insurance premiums** — 100% deductible above-the-line for sole proprietors (one of the most valuable deductions available; frequently unclaimed)

*CPA friction point:* Multi-platform income reconciliation is the most labour-intensive part of preparing returns for this segment. A CPA cannot begin preparing the return without totals from each platform — and each platform must be pulled manually if the client doesn't arrive with consolidated records.
